# Overcoming Shyness

## Key Takeaways

- Shyness is not introversion — introversion is where you get energy, shyness is the fear of being judged; you can be one without the other
- You're not shy around family or close friends, so shyness isn't who you are — specific people and high-stakes situations trigger it
- The situations that make you nervous are exactly where there's the most to gain — speaking up in those rooms is how careers accelerate
- Shyness behaves like a fitness level — it atrophies with disuse and strengthens with practice
- Most "high-stakes" situations aren't high-stakes at all — everyone forgets about it a day later

## Actionable Insights

**Recognize the disguises:**

- "I'm introverted" — cover for not introducing yourself; introverts can still speak up, they just recharge alone afterward
- "I'm being respectful" — cover for not reaching out; you can be direct and still give people an easy out
- "I'm humble" — cover for not taking credit; humble people own what they've done without overselling

**Build the courage muscle — start low-stakes, then escalate:**

- Ask the barista how their day is going
- Make a brief comment to someone in an elevator
- Try ideas out on friends before bringing them to bigger rooms
- Post a thought on LinkedIn — writing is still putting yourself out there
- Send a thoughtful message to someone senior with your take
- Ask for the referral

**Shyness has a half-life** — the longer you wait, the bigger it grows. At any new event or dinner, say hi to someone immediately. Waiting an hour makes it infinitely harder. Acting fast keeps fear from calcifying.

**Preparation beats shyness** — most of the fear comes from not knowing how it will go. Have an intro ready: a sharp line about what you do so you come across as someone worth knowing.
