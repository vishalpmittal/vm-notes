# Data Storytelling for Leaders

## Key Takeaways

- Numbers alone don't drive action — character-driven narratives release oxytocin (Paul Zak's research), fostering trust and cooperation; a slide showing a 12-point retention drop does not
- Jerome Bruner identifies two thinking modes: paradigmatic (logic) and narrative (meaning) — most dashboards only speak the first language while audiences need the second
- The person who controls a metric's narrative frame controls the team's response — framing a retention drop as a "technical failure" vs. a "customer relationship problem" activates entirely different responses
- Authenticity matters — teams distrust leaders who only tell positive stories or turn every metric into a crisis

## Actionable Insights

**Use the Story Frame** — three sentences before presenting any metric:

1. **The Before:** What was expected ("We planned for 80% retention through month 6")
2. **The Reveal:** What actually happened ("We're at 62%, with the steepest drop at month 3")
3. **The So What:** Why it matters and what to do ("Month-3 is where customers finish onboarding and hit real workflows — we're losing them at the value-delivery moment")

**Read metrics as relationship stories:**

- Week 1 drops → onboarding failure (first impression problem)
- Month 3–6 fade → insufficient value delivery (engagement problem)
- Month 12 cliffs → renewal/relationship breakdown (trust problem)

Each pattern has a hero (customer), villain (friction point), and guide (product/team).

**Try this:** Pick one metric you own. Write three Story Frame sentences. Present it that way in your next review and notice how the discussion shifts from "what happened?" to "what does this mean for who we are?"
