# Memorable Is Useable: Strategy That Sticks

## Key Takeaways

- **Strategic initiatives only drive behavior when people can actually retain them.** If your strategy doesn't fit in working memory, it won't influence the thousand small decisions made without you in the room
- The common failure mode: leadership teams craft elaborate strategies (**6 pillars, 14 objectives**, OKR cascades) that nobody can recite three months later
- When people can't recall the strategy, they **default to instinct, manager preference, or whatever feels urgent** — the strategy stops shaping decisions even when nominally "in effect"
- The leader's actual job: **create constraint structures that shape decisions** — and that requires constraints simple enough to be carried in heads
- **Test retention three months later.** If your team can't recite the goals, the strategy isn't doing its job

## Actionable Insights

### Ruthlessly Constrain Complexity
- Pick **3-5 strategic priorities, maximum** — anything more and people will remember different subsets
- Cut anything that doesn't fit in working memory. If you can't fit it in 7±2 items, edit
- "Comprehensive" strategy documents are mostly performative — useful for the people who wrote them, useless to the people who need to act on them

### Test Retention Explicitly
- Three months after launching the strategy, ask 10 random team members: **"What are our top priorities this quarter?"**
- If you get 10 different answers, the strategy has failed regardless of what's in the document
- Run this check periodically, not as a gotcha — as a diagnostic

### Align What Sticks
- If 14 values exist, **people will remember different subsets** of them — and act on different ones
- Standardize what to remember. Three core values that everyone recites the same way beat 14 values that nobody can fully list
- Make the language exactly repeatable so it propagates verbatim

### Frame Goals as Decision Tools
- A goal that doesn't help someone decide between two options isn't useful
- Test each goal: **"If a team member faced choice X vs Y, would this goal tell them which to pick?"** If no, refine
- Decorative goals ("be the best") fail this test; decision-shaping goals ("ship to enterprise customers before consumer") pass it

## The Underlying Principle

> "A leader's job is creating constraint structures that shape decisions. Complexity kills utility; simplicity enables adoption."

Strategy isn't a document. It's the constraints carried in your team's heads that make decisions consistent when you're not there. If the constraints don't fit in heads, they don't constrain anything.

The choice is between:
- A **comprehensive** strategy that's accurate but unmemorable → unused
- A **simple** strategy that's slightly lossy but memorable → actually shapes behavior

The second wins every time on real-world impact.

## Where This Shows Up

- **OKR season** — when teams have 5+ objectives × 3 KRs each (15+ items), most of them are inert
- **Values rollouts** — orgs with 14 values produce employees who quote different subsets
- **Strategy memos** — the 20-page deck nobody re-reads vs the one-line mission everyone remembers
- **All-hands repetition** — leaders sometimes feel they're being repetitive; usually they're not yet repetitive *enough* for the message to stick
