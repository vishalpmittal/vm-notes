# IC-to-Manager Transition

## Key Takeaways
- Becoming a manager is a genuine career change, not a promotion — the engineer identity must be fully released, not set aside temporarily
- Managers face two compounding losses: (1) loss of tangible, visible output — your work becomes invisible process and people outcomes; (2) loss of technical expertise as you deliberately make your team better than yourself
- Resisting this grief produces toxic anti-patterns: hoarding interesting work, rewriting reports' code, competing with direct reports — all of which drive away top performers
- The key shift is rebuilding your success scorecard from scratch: count promotions you sponsored, projects that shipped smoothly, conflicts you resolved — not lines of code or features shipped
- Many new managers fail not from incompetence but from refusing to let go — they stay in IC mode while holding a manager title

## Actionable Insights

- **Name it as grief, not "adjustment."** Explicitly acknowledge the loss. Suppressing it produces the hoarding and rewriting behaviors that damage teams.
- **Rebuild your scorecard on Fridays.** Ask: "Did I help the team move faster? Did I remove a blocker? Did I invest in someone's growth?" — not "Did I ship something?"
- **Deliberately make your reports better than you.** That is the job. Getting credit for their growth is your new form of output.
- **When you feel the pull to take back technical work, treat it as a signal** — that's grief talking, not a business need.
- **Separate "staying technically sharp" (healthy) from "staying in the critical path of technical decisions" (harmful).** The first is personal development. The second blocks team ownership.
- **Build a new identity metric system** early. Without it, the default is measuring yourself against your old IC metrics — and you'll always feel like you're losing.

## Key Concepts

**The Two Losses**
1. *Loss of tangible output* — as a manager, your contributions become invisible. You facilitated a project, resolved a conflict, coached someone. None of it shows in a diff.
2. *Loss of expertise* — your former peers deepen specialization while you become a generalist. The gap widens deliberately.

**New Manager Anti-Patterns** (symptoms of unprocessed grief)
- Hoarding the interesting technical work instead of delegating it
- Rewriting or heavily editing reports' code
- Competing with direct reports for technical visibility

**The Career Change Frame**
Management is a parallel track with different craft, not an elevation of the engineering track. The skills that made you a great IC (deep focus, technical precision, individual execution) are not just "less emphasized" as a manager — they actively work against you if you keep defaulting to them.

> "The engineer you were has to go. Not get busier, not get a bit rusty. Go."

> "On a Friday I realized I had not written a meaningful line of code in weeks — yet I had stopped duplicate efforts, resolved conflicts, and improved team processes."
