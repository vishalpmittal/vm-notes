# Leading Senior Engineers

## Key Takeaways

- Leading senior engineers requires a complete inversion of how you led junior developers — technical superiority becomes irrelevant and attempting it undermines the talent you hired
- Your value as their manager is removing obstacles (ambiguity, politics, your own technical "how"), not adding solutions
- Provide the *what* and *why*; senior engineers own the *how* — dictating execution breaks the autonomy that justified hiring them
- Interruptions are a silent tax: the average knowledge worker takes 23 minutes to refocus after an interrupt; meeting time has grown 153% since 2020
- You control up to 70% of team engagement variance — your presence is either a shield or a bottleneck

## Actionable Insights

- **Kill ambiguity before it reaches the team** — convert vague directives into crisp problem statements with clear success criteria and explicit non-goals
- **Translate politics, don't absorb it** — if you absorb organizational friction entirely and say nothing, you become a dependency; if you share context and let engineers navigate, you build autonomy
- **Stop proposing technical solutions in reviews or planning** — ask questions that help the engineer articulate the tradeoffs instead; your job is to frame, not to solve
- **Audit your week for adds vs. removes** — for each recurring meeting or process you own, ask: "does this remove friction for my team or add it?"
- **Maintain enough technical depth to frame discussions** — you don't need to code the solution, but you need to be credible enough to ask the right questions and recognize good answers

## The Three Things Worth Removing

### 1. Ambiguity
Senior engineers make excellent decisions given crisp inputs — they stall on vague ones. Your job is to:
- Define the problem clearly, including what the team is *not* doing
- Kill competing priorities so engineers can focus
- Translate "the business wants X" into a specific problem statement with constraints

### 2. Politics
Organizational friction is noise. Senior engineers tune it out — but if you don't translate it, it leaks as confusion and misaligned work.
- Surface the relevant context (why leadership cares, what pressure exists)
- Don't insulate so much that engineers have no map of the terrain — they can navigate it if they understand it
- Avoid becoming the single point of contact for every cross-team question (bottleneck pattern)

### 3. Your Technical "How"
This is the hardest one to give up, especially for engineers-turned-managers.
- Proposing solutions in technical discussions signals distrust and short-circuits the engineer's reasoning process
- Ask: "What are the tradeoffs between options A and B?" — not "I think we should do A"
- Reserve technical opinions for when safety, compliance, or irreversibility is at stake

## The Inverted Value Equation

| Junior teams | Senior teams |
|---|---|
| Lead through technical knowledge | Lead through clarity and obstacle removal |
| Teach the *how* | Define the *what* and *why* |
| Correctness as the bar | Autonomy and ownership as the bar |
| Reduce uncertainty for them | Create space for them to resolve uncertainty |

> "Never tell people how to do things. Tell them what to do, and they will surprise you with their ingenuity." — Patton

> "It doesn't make sense to hire smart people and then tell them what to do." — Steve Jobs
