# Getting to Specifics in Product Strategy

## Key Takeaways

- Abstract binary framings ("wide vs. deep," "horizontal vs. vertical," "platform vs. point") create the illusion of strategy without doing the actual work
- High-altitude business debates let everyone sound smart while avoiding uncomfortable, specific customer knowledge
- The real question is always one level deeper: what will actually work for specific customers in specific situations
- Product shape (wide, deep, platform, point) follows organically from genuine customer insight — not the other way around
- No framework or metaphor can rescue product decisions made without specific customer understanding

## The Abstract Binary Trap

Common framings that *feel* strategic but dodge the real work:

- Wide vs. deep
- Horizontal vs. vertical
- Platform vs. point solution
- CAC vs. LTV optimization

These let teams perform strategy without ever naming a customer, a feature, or a frustration.

## Actionable Insights

- **Refuse abstract binaries** — when someone poses one, redirect: "Which features will resonate with which customers?"
- **Replace strategy debates with concrete questions** — what frustration have you directly witnessed? What drives actual purchase and retention?
- **Treat comfort with abstract framings as a warning sign** — it usually signals lack of real customer knowledge
- **Push for uncomfortable specificity** — over elegant generalizations
- **Audit your strategy conversations** — if you could swap the product/market and the discussion still works, you're too abstract

## The Altitude Problem

> "At that altitude, everyone gets to sound smart."

Abstraction enables performance over substance. The leader's job is to keep pulling the conversation back down to specifics — even when (especially when) it's awkward.

## Self-Test

Ask: *if I replaced the product name and market in this conversation, would anything I just said become wrong?* If no, you're operating on framings, not insight.

> "Get to the core of the thing. Then talk about that."
