# Nonverbal Communication and EQ

## Key Takeaways

- Your message includes facial expressions and body language — mismatches signal insincerity and dilute impact even when the words are right
- Awareness of your unconscious "tells" (jaw clench, eye widening, lip pressing) is half the battle in regulating them
- Aligning words, tone, face, and posture creates psychological safety and invites honest dialogue
- Default head position should be straight (authoritative); tilt only to signal openness or listening
- Slowing your breath physiologically calms you, which in turn calms the room — a real-time reset for high-stakes moments

## Actionable Insights

- **Audit your tells** — during stressful conversations, notice unconscious behaviors that leak nervousness or doubt; consciously neutralize them
- **Match face to message** — soften face when delivering hard news; reduce expressions to project authority; raise brows and lean in to show listening
- **Monitor reactions when receiving feedback** — your face can shut a speaker down before you've heard them
- **Use head position deliberately** — straight for authority, slight tilt for warmth or curiosity
- **Slow breathing as a reset** — before and during high-stakes conversations; calm body produces calm room

## Core Frameworks

- **Tells (poker analogy)** — unconscious behavioral changes that reveal nerves or confidence
- **Signal-matching** — aligning content with tone, face, and posture for credibility; mismatches diminish your point
- **Psychological safety via body language** — calm body → calm brain → honest conversation
- **Head position as authority signal** — straight = serious, tilted = non-threatening

## Why It Matters

> "When your facial expressions and body language don't match your message, you diminish your ability to convey your point."

> "If you're the most powerful person in the room, you don't have to give other people clues."

Verbal craft (covered in [managing-up.md](managing-up.md)) is necessary but not sufficient. Nonverbal alignment is what makes a message land — or fail to.
