# Compensation and Promotion Strategy

How tech compensation actually grows — and why optimizing for early salary or fastest promotion is usually the wrong move. Philip Su's career arc (Microsoft → Facebook → OpenAI) makes the mechanics concrete.

## Key Takeaways

- **Mid-career compensation dwarfs early-career compensation by an order of magnitude.** Su went from ~$54k all-in at Microsoft (year 1) to ~$2.5M at OpenAI (year 25). Optimizing for a $10k entry-level bump is the wrong lever
- **Compensation bands overlap across levels** at most major tech companies. An exceptional E4 routinely out-earns a mid-pack E5 — so being *the best at your level* beats *getting to the next level fastest*
- **Comp growth is lumpy** ("punctuated equilibrium") — flat years, occasional pay cuts for skill investment, then large discontinuities at certain key levels (e.g., Microsoft's L67→L68 "Partner" where only 1% of employees land)
- **Geometric growth is the secret.** A fixed % raise on an exponentially larger base means most lifetime comp lands in years 15–25, not years 1–10
- **The Sally > Bob pattern:** the peer who gets promoted slower because she *consistently exceeds expectations* almost always out-earns the peer who gets promoted faster by *barely meeting them*

## Actionable Insights

### Don't Optimize the Entry-Level Offer

The Microsoft offer Su took was his second-lowest of his graduating cohort — but it gave him the best skill-building environment. Twenty years later the comp gap from that decision is ~$2M/year. **Pick the role that builds skills, not the one with the highest base.**

### Aim to Be Exceptional at Your Level, Not Fastest to the Next

| Path | What it looks like | Why it underperforms (or overperforms) |
|---|---|---|
| **Bob** — fast | E3→E5 in 4 years, "met expectations" reviews | Promotion-speed-optimized; small raises, average projects |
| **Sally** — slow | E3→E5 in 6 years, "greatly exceeded" reviews | Premium bonuses, premium projects, manager memory — compounds into a far larger long-term gap |

Tech rubrics reward *greatly exceeds expectations* far more than *meets expectations + new title*. Managers also remember outperformers and route the best projects to them, which compounds.

### The "Redshirting" Tactic

Su deliberately **delayed his L64→L65 promotion by six months** to stay in a pattern of overdelivering, rather than arriving at the new level underprepared and looking average. Sometimes the right move is *postponing* the promotion until you can show up exceeding the next level's bar.

### Expect (and Plan for) Flat Years

Comp isn't a smooth curve. Plan for years where nothing visible happens — the skill-building you do in those years is what unlocks the next discontinuity. Pay cuts to join a better-trajectory company can be the highest-ROI move you make.

### Target a "Best Self" Window of 35–40

Peak earning typically starts mid-30s. Optimize your 20s for *becoming someone worth paying* — choose roles for **maximum skill growth**, not maximum near-term comp.

### Mind the Outsized-Impact Promotions

Some level jumps reflect dramatically smaller talent pools and have outsized comp effects (Microsoft L66→L67 doubled RSUs; L67→L68 Partner = top 1%). When you're close to one of these, the extra effort to clear that specific bar pays back far more than evenly spaced raises would suggest.
