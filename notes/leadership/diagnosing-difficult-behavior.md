# Diagnosing Difficult Behavior

## Key Takeaways

- When similar complaints arise repeatedly across different people, the root cause is typically systemic, not personal — managers often fall prey to the fundamental attribution error
- Amy Edmondson's research: identical people produce different behaviors in different systems — high-performing teams reported *more* mistakes because members felt safe speaking up
- Three structural problems create most "difficult" behavior: ambiguous ownership, decisions that never close, and system-rewarded behavior
- Key test: if the same complaints appear with different people, it's structural
- Netflix culture memo: "company values are revealed by who gets rewarded, promoted, or tolerated"

## Actionable Insights

**Before your next difficult conversation about an engineer, check three things:**

**1. Is ownership actually clear?**

- Symptoms: engineers step into unclear domains, reopen decisions, sound territorial
- Fix: document who owns the service, who makes final calls, who handles incidents, who owns planning
- Will Larson: "many poor working relationships come from information gaps"

**2. Are decisions documented and closed?**

- Symptoms: engineers reopen discussions, escalate, challenge settled decisions
- Root cause: decisions get reversed by whoever pushes hardest, making escalation rational
- Fix: record what was decided, who decided, who was consulted, when it can be revisited
- Reinforce "disagree and commit" — discussion is open pre-decision, teams move forward together afterward

**3. Is the system rewarding this behavior?**

- Symptoms: someone everyone complains about persists despite conversations
- Root cause: they're fast, know critical systems, or receive praise from leadership regardless of behavior
- Fix: examine what actually gets rewarded, tolerated, or called out

**When it really is the person:** Ask "If the structure changed tomorrow, would this person adapt?" Most would. Those who won't signal a genuine individual issue — but fix the system first so you're addressing the real problem, not its symptom.
