# Spotting BS

## Key Takeaways

- Careers are long runs of decisions made on what others tell you — the ability to separate truth from confident-sounding nonsense compounds more than almost any other skill
- AI has made sounding smart free, so it's stopped being worth anything — the differentiator is now spotting fake fluency
- Most BS is engineered to pass a quick listen, not real inspection — first-principles reconstruction breaks it instantly
- Spotting BS is only half the skill; *calling* it requires leverage (another offer, a hard-to-replace skill, willingness to push back)
- The best BS detector is your own experience — question what you're told, test what you can, trust what you've seen

## Five Checks to Run on Any Claim

### 1. First principles — does it add up?

Ignore who's saying it and how slick it sounds. Rebuild the claim from scratch and ask what would have to be true for it to work.

- Example: someone promises 30% annual returns. Buffett averaged ~20% over his career. Either this person beats Buffett, or it's a Ponzi.
- Most BS rarely survives this question.

### 2. What do they gain if you believe them?

> "Whose bread I eat, his song I sing."

People argue for what pays them. Doesn't mean they're lying — just trust the claim less when they profit from it.

Ask: **What's their incentive? What's their expertise? What's their agenda?**

### 3. Does it survive reality?

Bezos was told in a meeting that Amazon support wait times were under a minute. Data confirmed it. He called support from the meeting — actual wait was 10+ minutes.

- When numbers and on-the-ground reality disagree, trust reality — someone chose what to measure and how
- A statistic is not proof; it's a measurement decision
- *Corollary:* when you're making the claim, real specific numbers are the strongest backup you can bring

> "When the data doesn't agree with the anecdote, the anecdote is usually right." — Bezos

### 4. Have they done this before?

If you can't check the claim, check the person's track record.

- Manager promising "promotion next quarter" for two years
- Startup "six months from profitable" for three years
- Client who'll "definitely pay next week" every week

Once a pattern is visible, stop extending the benefit of the doubt.

### 5. Listen for the tells

None is proof alone, but several together should raise alarms:

| Tell | What it signals |
|------|-----------------|
| **Exaggeration** — everything is amazing, huge, best ever | Real experts underplay; inflation suggests insecurity |
| **Vagueness** — adjectives instead of specifics | Specifics can be checked; BS avoids them |
| **Never says "I don't know"** | Real experts say it constantly |
| **Leads with credentials** | People with the goods show the goods, not the diploma |
| **The AI sheen** — smooth, evenly polished, sure about everything, curious about nothing | Fluent isn't the same as true |

## Actionable Insights

- **When you can't tell, test small** — don't argue, let reality answer. Cheap test beats confident opinion every time.
- **Build leverage in parallel with judgment** — "no budget for raises" becomes "we found you a 40% bump" the week you bring another offer. Seeing through BS does nothing without something behind it.
- **Audit your own claims** — would they survive a first-principles rebuild? Are you leading with credentials or substance? Do you say "I don't know" enough?
- **Stay calibrated about who's right about what** — being right about one thing doesn't make someone right about everything. Take a person seriously in their lane, then re-verify outside it.

## The Two-Halves Rule

> "Spotting BS is the first half. Calling it is the second, and calling it takes leverage."

Without leverage, you'll see the BS clearly — and still nod along. Build the skill and the leverage together.
