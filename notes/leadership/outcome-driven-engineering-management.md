# Outcome-Driven Engineering Management

## Key Takeaways

- The shift: from **"keep the team chugging along"** to **optimizing for measurable outcomes**. A functional team isn't enough — organizations now demand faster decisions, better delivery, maximum efficiency
- The framework — **People + Technology + Organization (Product & Business)** — must work like an airplane engine: every part contributes to one essential goal
- Combine **quantitative signals** (delivery predictability, cycle time, throughput, interruptions) with **qualitative insights** (clarity of priorities, decision-making ease, ownership, process friction). Neither alone is enough
- Common waste to eliminate: **activity without impact** (meetings, processes for their own sake), sluggish decisions, misaligned priorities, underutilized talent, silos, **"obsession over processes" without value**, over-engineering
- The core test for any activity: **does it produce tangible outcomes for the team?** If you can't answer, it's probably waste

## Actionable Insights

### Audit Yourself First
Walk through your weekly calendar. For each meeting, process, or ritual, ask:
- **Does this produce a tangible outcome?**
- If yes — which one, and how would I measure it?
- If no — why am I doing it?

Most leaders find 20-40% of their activities don't survive this test.

### Clarify the Outcomes Your Leadership Expects
Don't infer. Ask explicitly:
- What are the top 3 outcomes you want my team to drive this quarter?
- How will you measure success?
- What would "the team is failing" look like to you?

Misalignment between what your team optimizes for and what your leadership measures is the #1 source of "we worked hard and still got bad reviews."

### Identify Your Unconstrained Move
Ask yourself: **"If I had no constraints — political, organizational, technical — what would I optimize first?"** That answer tells you what you actually believe matters. Then negotiate for the constraints to relax.

### Combine Data With Conversation
Quantitative signals tell you *what's happening*. Qualitative conversations with your team tell you *why*. Both are required:

- Cycle time spiked → ask why in 1:1s
- Throughput dropped → ask how priorities feel
- Interruptions up → ask which kinds (PagerDuty? leadership asks? cross-team blocking?)

Numbers without context lead to wrong fixes. Conversations without numbers lead to missed problems.

### The "Why" Test for Processes
For every process your team runs (standups, sprint planning, retros, design reviews, etc.):
- What outcome does this drive?
- What would happen if we stopped doing it for a month?

Many processes outlived their original purpose. The team kept doing them because nobody said to stop.

### Watch for These Wastes
| Waste | Symptom |
|---|---|
| **Activity without impact** | "We're so busy!" but ship velocity hasn't changed |
| **Sluggish decisions** | Proposals sit for weeks awaiting "alignment" |
| **Misaligned priorities** | Team is grinding on something leadership doesn't care about |
| **Underutilized talent** | Strong engineer doing maintenance work indefinitely |
| **Silos** | Same problem solved 3 times in 3 teams |
| **Process obsession** | More meetings about how to work than time actually working |
| **Over-engineering** | 6-month design for a 2-month feature; abstractions for use cases that never materialize |

### The Three Components — People, Technology, Organization

Like an airplane engine, the components must work together:

| Component | What's in it | Failure mode |
|---|---|---|
| **People** | Team composition, skills, motivation, growth | Burnout, talent loss, capability gaps |
| **Technology** | Codebase, infrastructure, tooling, technical debt | Slow delivery, brittle systems, debugging fatigue |
| **Organization** | Priorities, decision-making, processes, product alignment | Wrong work being done well |

Engineering leaders who only focus on technology (the easiest one to control) often fail because People or Organization issues dominate the outcomes.

## The Underlying Principle

> Optimize for outcomes, not activity.

It's simple to state, hard to live. Activity is satisfying — meetings happen, code gets written, PRs merge. Outcomes are harder to measure and slower to manifest. The discipline is in continuously asking "what outcome did this activity drive?" and being willing to kill activities that can't answer.
