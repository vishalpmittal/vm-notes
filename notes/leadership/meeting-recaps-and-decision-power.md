# Meeting Recaps and Decision Power

A decision isn't real until it's written down — and the person who writes it down decides what was decided.

## Key Takeaways

- Meetings end fuzzier than people realize. Everyone leaves with a slightly different version of "what we agreed" — the recap is what collapses those versions into one
- The recap author **frames the decision, owns the action items, and seeds the next agenda** — without needing more political capital than it takes to type
- For juniors: writing notes looks like a low-status job, which is why no one fights you for it — making it an asymmetric influence move
- For managers: skipping the recap means you'll re-litigate the same decisions in three weeks, because each attendee's recollection drifted toward what suits them
- A decision you "got verbally" is not yet a decision. Writing it down is the act that converts intent into commitment

## Actionable Insights

- **Volunteer to write the recap.** Especially in meetings where you're the most junior person — it's the cheapest source of leverage in the room
- **Send within the day.** Recaps lose power once people's memories diverge further; first written version wins
- **Use the standard frame:** *Decisions / Owners / Open questions / Things to explore next*. The "things to explore next" line is where you can quietly put your own ideas on the official agenda
- **Make decisions explicit, not implied.** "We agreed X" not "We discussed X" — the difference is whether it sticks
- **Name owners by person, not team.** "Marketing will handle the launch plan" diffuses; "Priya will own the launch plan" sticks
- **If you're the manager, don't outsource the recap to chance.** Either write it yourself or explicitly assign it — and read the recap before it goes out
