# How Your Work Gets Judged

Every important career outcome — raise, promotion, reputation — is decided by other people based on their impression of your work. Impressions form through predictable quirks of human judgment, and those quirks regularly make good work look worth less than it is. The locksmith parable: an apprentice spent an hour opening jammed locks, struggled visibly, got paid full fee + tip. Years later, two minutes per lock — better work in every way — and customers complained about the price.

## Key Takeaways

- **Visible effort is what gets valued, not skill.** Mastery hides the years of work that made an easy delivery possible — name the difficulty out loud or get credited for nothing
- **Recency dominates memory.** A whole year of work gets compressed to the last three weeks at review time. Time your asks and keep a win log
- **Loss framing beats gain framing roughly 2:1** (Kahneman & Tversky). Pitches land harder when they name what's being lost by inaction
- **Incentives override stated values, every time.** What the paycheck/promotion rewards is the actual rule book — official instructions lose
- **Decision quality follows physical state.** Halbert's HALT rule — never decide while Hungry, Angry, Lonely, Tired. You can't tell if you're thinking clearly, but you can always tell if you're tired

## Quirk 1 — The Labor Illusion

People judge the effort they can see. Travel sites animate "searching 500 airlines..." because instant results feel cheap. AI feels more trustworthy with visible "thinking" time. Skill punishes you here: as you get faster, difficult work looks easy, and people quietly lower their estimate of its value.

**Actionable insights:**
- When you deliver something hard, name the difficulty in one sentence: *"This looked simple but the data was a mess — three rebuilds before it held."*
- Stop the reflexive *"oh, it was nothing"* when praised. Try: *"It was a grind — glad it landed."*
- For peers and reports, make their hidden work visible publicly — same principle, applied as leadership

## Quirk 2 — Recency Bias in Reviews

Your manager answers "how did this person do this year?" from memory at review time, and memory massively overweights the last few weeks. A strong year ending in one bad month gets remembered as a bad year.

**Actionable insights:**
- **Time your asks.** Raise/promo/big-project requests go right after a visible win. Just made a mistake? Wait, ship the next win, then ask
- **Keep a weekly win log.** Five minutes every Friday — what you finished, fixed, or handled. Bring the list to your review so the conversation is about the actual year, not the last three weeks
- Doubles as a resume-update artifact

## Quirk 3 — Loss Aversion in Framing

Losing $100 feels about twice as bad as gaining $100 feels good. Most proposals can be framed as a gain or a loss — the loss version is far more persuasive.

| Gain frame (weak) | Loss frame (strong) |
|---|---|
| "This tool could help us win more clients" | "We're losing about two deals a month to competitors who already use this" |
| "Investing in tests will speed us up" | "We're burning ~20% of every sprint on regressions we shipped last quarter" |

**Actionable insights:**
- Before any pitch, ask: *what are we losing right now by not doing this?* Lead with that
- Keep the loss **true and specific** — invented losses destroy trust faster than weak gain framing does
- Works in reverse too: if someone's pitching you a "nice-to-have," ask them to name the cost of waiting

## Quirk 4 — Incentives Beat Instructions

Every workplace has two direction sets — official (instructions, stated values, what the boss asks for) and real (what actually pays, promotes, or earns praise). When they conflict, people follow the real set, usually without realizing it. Xerox released a better, cheaper printer; the old one kept outselling it because salesmen earned higher commission on the old one.

**Actionable insights:**
- When someone's behavior confuses you, **find what they're rewarded for.** The infra team blocking your launch? They're probably judged on stability — your launch is pure risk. Offer a gradual rollout and the resistance evaporates
- Want creativity from your team? Stop praising only finished work — finished-but-safe is what you'll get
- Ask for problems to be flagged early, but visibly annoyed when they are? You've trained the team to hide them
- To learn a company's real values, **skip the values page and look at what the last three promoted people actually did**

## Quirk 5 — Your Own Brain (HALT)

The other quirks sit in other people's heads where you can spot them. Your own quirks are harder — a bad decision feels reasonable while you're making it. What you *can* see is your physiological state.

**Gary Halbert's HALT rule:** never decide anything while **H**ungry, **A**ngry, **L**onely, or **T**ired.

**Actionable insights:**
- Sleep on big decisions; send the angry email tomorrow (you usually won't send it)
- Move negotiations and important calls off exhausting days — tired people accept bad terms just to end the conversation
- Eat before hard 1:1s
