# How Your Work Gets Judged

Every important career outcome — raise, promotion, reputation — is decided by other people based on their impression of your work. Impressions form through predictable quirks of human judgment, and those quirks regularly make good work look worth less than it is. The locksmith parable: an apprentice spent an hour opening jammed locks, struggled visibly, got paid full fee + tip. Years later, two minutes per lock — better work in every way — and customers complained about the price.

## Key Takeaways

- **Visible effort is what gets valued, not skill.** Mastery hides the years of work that made an easy delivery possible — name the difficulty out loud or get credited for nothing
- **Recency dominates memory.** A whole year of work gets compressed to the last three weeks at review time. Time your asks and keep a win log
- **Loss framing beats gain framing roughly 2:1** (Kahneman & Tversky). Pitches land harder when they name what's being lost by inaction
- **Incentives override stated values, every time.** What the paycheck/promotion rewards is the actual rule book — official instructions lose
- **Decision quality follows physical state.** Halbert's HALT rule — never decide while Hungry, Angry, Lonely, Tired. You can't tell if you're thinking clearly, but you can always tell if you're tired
- **Quiet excellence no longer guarantees recognition.** Remote + AI-assisted work makes output harder to observe; deliberate visible behaviors (proactive broadcasting, options + recommendation, scope creation) are what advance you
- **Reliability buys trust to execute — not trust to decide.** Delivery trust and strategic standing are completely separate currencies earned through different actions; one never converts to the other automatically. Growing scope without growing decision authority is the trap, not a sign of progress
- **Managers form a working performance draft by mid-cycle.** The year-end self-review fills information gaps, not forms opinions. Output (work done) vs. outcome (measurable business impact) is the gap that separates average from high ratings — long hours are never the argument a manager makes for you in calibration

## Quirk 1 — The Labor Illusion

People judge the effort they can see. Travel sites animate "searching 500 airlines..." because instant results feel cheap. AI feels more trustworthy with visible "thinking" time. Skill punishes you here: as you get faster, difficult work looks easy, and people quietly lower their estimate of its value.

**Actionable insights:**
- When you deliver something hard, name the difficulty in one sentence: *"This looked simple but the data was a mess — three rebuilds before it held."*
- Stop the reflexive *"oh, it was nothing"* when praised. Try: *"It was a grind — glad it landed."*
- For peers and reports, make their hidden work visible publicly — same principle, applied as leadership

## Quirk 2 — Recency Bias in Reviews

Your manager answers "how did this person do this year?" from memory at review time, and memory massively overweights the last few weeks. A strong year ending in one bad month gets remembered as a bad year.

**Actionable insights:**
- **Time your asks.** Raise/promo/big-project requests go right after a visible win. Just made a mistake? Wait, ship the next win, then ask
- **Keep a weekly win log.** Five minutes every Friday — what you finished, fixed, or handled. Bring the list to your review so the conversation is about the actual year, not the last three weeks
- Doubles as a resume-update artifact

## Quirk 3 — Loss Aversion in Framing

Losing $100 feels about twice as bad as gaining $100 feels good. Most proposals can be framed as a gain or a loss — the loss version is far more persuasive.

| Gain frame (weak) | Loss frame (strong) |
|---|---|
| "This tool could help us win more clients" | "We're losing about two deals a month to competitors who already use this" |
| "Investing in tests will speed us up" | "We're burning ~20% of every sprint on regressions we shipped last quarter" |

**Actionable insights:**
- Before any pitch, ask: *what are we losing right now by not doing this?* Lead with that
- Keep the loss **true and specific** — invented losses destroy trust faster than weak gain framing does
- Works in reverse too: if someone's pitching you a "nice-to-have," ask them to name the cost of waiting

## Quirk 4 — Incentives Beat Instructions

Every workplace has two direction sets — official (instructions, stated values, what the boss asks for) and real (what actually pays, promotes, or earns praise). When they conflict, people follow the real set, usually without realizing it. Xerox released a better, cheaper printer; the old one kept outselling it because salesmen earned higher commission on the old one.

**Actionable insights:**
- When someone's behavior confuses you, **find what they're rewarded for.** The infra team blocking your launch? They're probably judged on stability — your launch is pure risk. Offer a gradual rollout and the resistance evaporates
- Want creativity from your team? Stop praising only finished work — finished-but-safe is what you'll get
- Ask for problems to be flagged early, but visibly annoyed when they are? You've trained the team to hide them
- To learn a company's real values, **skip the values page and look at what the last three promoted people actually did**

## Quirk 5 — Your Own Brain (HALT)

The other quirks sit in other people's heads where you can spot them. Your own quirks are harder — a bad decision feels reasonable while you're making it. What you *can* see is your physiological state.

**Gary Halbert's HALT rule:** never decide anything while **H**ungry, **A**ngry, **L**onely, or **T**ired.

**Actionable insights:**
- Sleep on big decisions; send the angry email tomorrow (you usually won't send it)
- Move negotiations and important calls off exhausting days — tired people accept bad terms just to end the conversation
- Eat before hard 1:1s

## Visible Behaviors That Signal Next-Level Readiness

Knowing how judgment works is half the equation. The other half: deliberately demonstrating behaviors that register as "next-level" to the people making the call.

> "As more work goes remote and more output gets assisted by AI, the people who advance will be the ones who over-index on visible leadership behavior."

Observable behavior determines advancement more than tenure or raw output. Quiet excellence is insufficient when a manager can't see it.

### The 7 Behaviors

| Behavior | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| **Arrive with options + a recommendation** | Don't ask "what should I do?" — bring 3 options with your preferred choice already stated |
| **Broadcast progress proactively** | Share wins and risks before being asked; eliminate your manager's need to chase status |
| **Make people around you better** | Actively develop teammates; create upward force on the whole team's output |
| **Connect work to business outcomes** | Frame every task in terms of org impact, not just technical delivery |
| **Progress through ambiguity** | Move forward when requirements are unclear; don't stall waiting for perfect specs |
| **Create your own scope** | Identify and initiate work beyond assigned tasks; find unowned problems |
| **Recognize "good enough"** | Stop when additional polish adds no value; perfectionism is a career tax |

### Anti-Patterns to Eliminate

- **Wrong communication altitude** — dumping full technical depth on execs, or under-explaining to engineers. Adjust per audience
- **Visible irritation** — sighs, eye rolls, dismissive comments in meetings. Transform frustration into constructive questions
- **Firefighter trap** — constant crisis-response locks you in reactive mode and prevents strategic visibility. Reactivity reads as junior

### Action

Pick one behavior. Engineer a situation in the next 7 days where your manager will clearly observe it.

## The Reliable Team Trap

Reliable, high-performing teams attract more execution work — more scope, more projects, more trust to deliver. This feels like recognition. It isn't. The reward for execution is more execution.

**The core distinction:** Delivery trust (your boss trusts you to execute their vision) vs. strategic standing (your boss trusts your judgment to set direction). These are earned through completely different mechanisms, and one never converts to the other automatically.

Without visible strategic judgment, your boss must set direction for your area by default. More scope under those conditions is a trap, not a sign of progress.

> "Reliability buys you trust to execute. It does not buy you trust to make the decision." — Kevin Goldsmith

### The Trap Cycle

Success → more scope → less capacity → no time for strategy → invisible judgment → continued execution. The cycle self-reinforces.

**Signal you're in it:** your manager interactions are mostly output reports, not judgment calls. Your role expands but your decision authority doesn't.

### The Four Moves to Break Out

These are deliberate, separate acts from doing the work:

1. **Show the Thinking** — in every 1-on-1, share one decision and its trade-off reasoning, not just status. This is what strategic standing looks like to your manager.
2. **Build the Bench** — delegate at least one recurring task only you currently handle. Done slower by someone else is still done; done only by you is a ceiling.
3. **Claim the Contribution** — write 2-3 sentences quarterly linking your team's work to business outcomes and share them with your boss and peers. The connection won't be made for you.
4. **Take a Position** — form a written opinion on one domain trend's 6-12 month implications and make it visible. Judgment that lives only in your head is strategically invisible.

**Monthly time audit:** classify each hour as delivery vs. direction. If direction is near zero, that's the gap. If last month's manager interactions were mostly output reporting, shift deliberately.

## The Manager's View at Calibration

Managers don't form their performance assessments at year-end — they form working drafts by mid-cycle. Mid-year check-ins and milestone reviews are the real opinion-forming events. The year-end self-review fills in details and ammunition, not the overall picture.

> "Managers rate what they can see — filling information gaps with assumptions otherwise."

### Output vs. Outcome

This is the core gap that separates average from high ratings:

| Output | Outcome |
|---|---|
| "Completed the refactor" | "Completed the refactor, saving 2 hours per dev cycle per sprint" |
| "Fixed the bug" | "Fixed the bug, eliminating the #1 support escalation path" |
| "Shipped the feature" | "Shipped the feature; three enterprise accounts upgraded within 30 days" |

Output descriptions are what most engineers share. Outcome descriptions are what managers can actually use when defending a rating against a peer in calibration.

**Note:** Long hours are never the argument a manager makes for you. Overtime is read as poor prioritization or inefficiency, not dedication. Strong performers create sustainable solutions.

### Calibration Mechanics

Calibration is where managers peer-review each other's ratings. Your manager needs concrete examples to defend your rating when challenged. Minimal 1-on-1 participation means they have nothing to quote. **One sentence of context after stating a completion** — the technical difficulty, the coordination complexity, what you unblocked — is the content managers quote in calibration meetings.

### Promotion Timing

Promotion conversations started at cycle-end arrive too late. Managers need runway (months) to create qualifying opportunities: mentorship assignments, cross-team scope, stretch projects. The right time to ask "what does this path require from your perspective?" is at cycle start, not the end.

**Actionable:**
- Once per quarter in a 1-on-1: ask directly "What's your current assessment of my performance?" — not just at review time
- Every status update: close with a concrete business impact sentence
- Every 1-on-1 update: add one sentence of context about difficulty, coordination, or what you unblocked
- At cycle start: "What does the promotion path require from your perspective this cycle?"
