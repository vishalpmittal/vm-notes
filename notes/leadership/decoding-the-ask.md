# Decoding the Ask

## Key Takeaways

- Every infrequent or mysterious meeting has a reason — assume someone has a specific ask for you, even if it isn't surfaced explicitly
- When someone schedules ambiguous time, lead with "How can I help?" — demonstrating availability matters more than immediately decoding the ask
- For cross-team asks, never say yes in the room; explain your team's planning cycle and history first, and expect a second meeting
- Senior leadership is a long game — relationships that look pointless (recurring 1:1s with no clear deliverable) often pay off years later through unexpected collisions
- Much of senior leadership is gut-driven instinct built from trial and error, not deductive logic

## The Three Assumptions

When you walk into an unfamiliar meeting, assume:

1. **The meeting exists for a reason** — even if the invite is vague
2. **You're not decoration** — your presence is intentional
3. **Someone will deliver an ask** — your job is to surface it gracefully

## Meeting Archetypes

- **The Promotion Conversation** — ambiguous 1:1 where the ask is career-related; surface it with "How can I help?" rather than waiting for the other person to find courage
- **The We-Need-You-(Your-Team) Meeting** — cross-team ask requiring context on your planning model before commitment; never agree in the room
- **The Shared Fate Meeting** — long-horizon relationship 1:1s with no immediate deliverable; payoff comes years later

## Actionable Insights

- **Don't decline calendar mysteries** — show up curious and listen for the ask
- **Open ambiguous 1:1s with "How can I help?"** instead of waiting for the requester to articulate
- **For new-team requests** — explain your planning cycle, resource allocation, and historical collaboration patterns *before* agreeing to anything
- **Protect shared-fate 1:1s** — they're the first things dropped when calendar gets crowded, but they're strategic, not nice-to-have
- **Trust gut on who should be talking** — act on instinct about useful relationships even without a concrete deliverable

## Why It Matters

> "You might not get to The Ask. That's ok. There needs to be another meeting."

> "It's not guessing — it's instinct built from endless trial and error."

Senior leadership operates on pattern recognition, not deduction. The willingness to invest in low-information meetings is what builds that pattern library.
