# Leading Through Executive Conflict

## Key Takeaways

- Executive conflict that lands on a project lead is usually a **structural** problem (fractured governance, unclear decision rights) — not a personality problem to solve with diplomacy
- Capable leaders default to "invisible heroics" (privately reconciling contradictions, softening messages) which masks dysfunction and creates dependency on one person's political skill
- Shift the conversation from **emotion to enterprise risk** — senior stakeholders tolerate interpersonal friction longer than they tolerate visible delivery exposure
- Replace informal coordination with **forced alignment mechanisms**: steering forums, written decision logs, pre-defined escalation routes
- Make a written "Conditions for Success" agreement — if executives won't supply the conditions, you've learned something essential about the organization's real commitment

## The Situation

A project lead is running a strategically critical initiative where two or more senior sponsors are in conflict — issuing contradictory direction, withdrawing resources unpredictably, and forcing the leader into an exhausting unofficial mediator role. Reframe this as a **governance and authority problem**, not an interpersonal one.

## Actionable Insights

- **Reframe escalation language** — instead of "mixed messages" / "politics are impossible" / "I'm stuck in the middle," use: *"Conflicting executive direction is creating execution ambiguity and delivery risk."*
- **Install forced-alignment mechanisms** — formal steering meetings with both sponsors present, written decision logs, explicit priority reviews, shared milestone governance, pre-defined escalation routes
- **Stop being the shock absorber** — refuse to act on contradictory direction without clarification; document decisions explicitly; escalate risks earlier; make trade-offs visible rather than privately compensating through overwork
- **Distinguish task conflict from relationship conflict** — once it's relational between sponsors, stop trying to repair it; manage *around* it
- **Treat resistance to alignment mechanisms as data** — if execs resist them, ambiguity may be serving someone's political interests

## Conditions for Success Document

A written agreement covering:

- Decisions required in next 30 / 60 / 90 days
- Named owners for budget, resources, scope, escalation
- Trade-offs that need resolution
- Risks created by current ambiguity
- Support needed from each sponsor
- Consequences if conditions are not met

## Coaching Questions

**Clarifying decision rights:**
- When strategic priorities conflict, who holds final decision authority?
- Who owns commercial, budgetary, and resource commitments?
- What escalation mechanism exists when executives provide contradictory direction?
- Which decisions rest with the project lead vs. requiring sponsor agreement?

**Reframing the personal challenge:**
- Instead of "How do I help these two work better together?" — ask: *"Given the relationship as it currently exists, what leadership choices best protect delivery, the team, and my own effectiveness?"*

**Closing reflection (the inflection point):**
1. What am I currently absorbing that should properly be visible to the wider system?
2. Have I accepted accountability for outcomes without the authority needed to shape conditions for success?
3. If nothing materially changes in the next three months, is this still a leadership challenge I can responsibly own?

**The deeper question:**
- *What must be true for this work to succeed, and who has the authority to make those conditions real?*
- At what point does professionalism become unhealthy accommodation?

## Why It Matters

> "Professionalism does not require indefinite endurance. The ultimate leadership challenge is recognizing when healthy resilience becomes unhealthy accommodation."

> "Effective leaders do not escalate emotion — they escalate consequences."
