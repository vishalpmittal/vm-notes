# From Technical Leader to Business Leader

## Key Takeaways

- Senior technical leaders hit a career ceiling not from technical gaps, but from lack of business fluency — the ability to frame technology in commercial terms
- The shift: stop protecting engineering from business realities, start representing business interests within engineering
- Business leadership demands selecting the right trade-off for company goals, not finding the technically correct answer
- Seniority creates expectations of fluency but doesn't automatically develop it — you must build it deliberately

## Actionable Insights

**Four moves of business fluency:**

1. **Translate** — Convert technical statements into business consequences. "Scaling limits in our pipeline" → "we can handle 30% growth before pausing features for a quarter to rebuild"
2. **Trade-off** — Present decisions as competing alternatives with costs, not recommendations. Show the shape of the decision before advocating
3. **Commit** — Deliver on business decisions even when disagreeing. Relitigating lost decisions signals immaturity at executive levels
4. **Compound** — Build fluency through ongoing practice, not as a one-time effort

**Four contexts to build fluency in:**

- **P&L and unit economics** — understand revenue lines, cost structures, gross margins, and industry benchmarks for your function
- **Sales and customer work** — sit in on customer calls; understand actual buying behavior vs. assumed needs; learn how compensation structures drive priorities
- **Fundraising and investor relations** — frame technology risks as bounded business concerns; investor questions reveal their underlying worries
- **Strategy and resource allocation** — develop business perspectives on market entry, competitive dynamics, and organizational priorities beyond technical implications

**Self-assessment:** Review your last three executive meetings. If technical contributions vastly outnumber commercial ones, spend real time in one business context this quarter — sit in meetings, read the documents, ask foundational questions.
