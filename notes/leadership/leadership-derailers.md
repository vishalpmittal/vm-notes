# Leadership Derailers (Marshall Goldsmith)

The behaviors that helped you succeed early in your career often **become the things that hold you back** as a leader. Most leadership failure isn't a skill gap — it's a habit you no longer notice.

> Source book: *What Got You Here Won't Get You There* — Marshall Goldsmith

## Key Takeaways

- **The Superstition Trap:** *"I behave this way → I am successful → therefore I must be successful because I behave this way."* Confusing correlation with causation about your own behavior is the root error. Some of your habits drove your success; others happened *despite* your behavior, not because of it
- The 21 derailing habits cluster into five themes: **ego-defense, communication failures, withholding, blame-shifting, and stuck patterns** — most senior leaders display at least 3–4 without realizing
- The fixes are unglamorous: **stop, apologize, listen, thank, follow up.** That's most of the book in one line
- The harder the habit is to see in yourself, the more visible it is to everyone around you. **Get feedback from people who report to you, not from peers**
- "Adding too much value" — the well-meaning trap of improving every idea your team brings you — is the most common derailer for promoted engineers

## The Superstition Trap

The single insight that frames the whole book:

> "I behave this way. I am successful. Therefore, I must be successful because I behave this way."

This conflates **correlation with causation** about your own behavior. You can be successful *and* have a destructive habit at the same time — the habit isn't fuel, it's a tax you're paying that you stopped noticing.

The senior-leader version: the more successful you become, the more reluctant you are to change *anything* about how you work, because "it's been working." That's exactly when the cost of the habits compounds the most.

## The 21 Destructive Habits

Grouped by theme for easier diagnosis:

### Ego-defense (you signaling your status)

- **Winning too much** — having to win in every conversation, every meeting, every casual disagreement
- **Adding too much value** — taking someone's idea and improving it by 5% while reducing their ownership by 50%. The classic promoted-engineer trap
- **Telling the world how smart we are** — needing others to know we already knew
- **Excessive need to be me** — defending "that's just how I am" instead of changing it
- **Taking undeserved credit** — overstating your role in a shared win
- **Claiming credit we don't deserve** — same family, more subtle versions

### Communication failures

- **Starting responses with "No," "But," or "However"** — every sentence that begins with these tells the other person you weren't listening
- **Making destructive comments** — needless sarcasm, cutting remarks, "just kidding" jabs
- **Speaking when angry** — emotion as a control tactic; you can't take it back
- **Not listening** — the most passive-aggressive way to disrespect someone in the room
- **Passing judgment** — rating people's ideas in real-time, even when they didn't ask
- **Negativity** — "let me explain why that won't work" as the reflex response

### Withholding

- **Withholding information** — keeping people in the dark to preserve a power asymmetry
- **Failure to recognize** — not noticing, or not saying, when someone did something well
- **Failing to express gratitude** — assuming people know you appreciate them
- **Refusing to express regret** — never apologizing, ever, for anything

### Blame / avoidance

- **Punishing the messenger** — making the person who brought you bad news pay for it; great way to ensure no one tells you the truth again
- **Passing the buck** (blaming others) — never your fault
- **Making excuses** — having a reason for every miss, even when nobody asked

### Stuck patterns

- **Clinging to the past** — telling the same war stories, defending decisions made under different conditions
- **Playing favorites** — rewarding people who flatter you, not people who perform
- **Goal obsession** — pursuing a goal so single-mindedly that you damage everything around it. The endpoint becomes the only thing that exists

## Actionable Insights — The Goldsmith Intervention

The fixes are simple, hard to do, and almost embarrassingly unglamorous:

- **Stop trying to win every conversation.** Most aren't competitions. Ask: *"Is this worth winning?"* before responding
- **Stop "adding value."** When a report brings you an idea, let it ship as theirs — even if you'd have done it differently. Their ownership matters more than your 5% improvement
- **Apologize.** Once, sincerely, specifically. Then move on. Don't argue the apology
- **Catch yourself starting sentences with "No / But / However."** Replace with "Yes, and..." or just listen first
- **Thank people.** Specifically and often. Then ask them what *you* could do better
- **Solicit feedback from reports, not peers.** Peers will protect you. Reports see what you actually do
- **Feedforward, not feedback** — ask "what could I do differently in the future?" instead of "what did I do wrong?" — the future framing avoids defensiveness
- **Follow up.** The single most important behavior. Ask the same people again in 3–6 months. Sustained behavior change is what actually moves the perception of you, not a one-time effort

## How to Spot Your Own Derailers

You won't see them by introspection — they're invisible from inside. Two practical approaches:

- **The 360 review with reports** — anonymous, specific, repeated annually
- **Recurring complaints** — when the same critique shows up across multiple unrelated people, the habit is real (even if every individual instance feels defensible)
