# Candidate-Side Interviewing

Most interview advice is for the interviewer. This note is the inverse: **questions a senior candidate should ask** to evaluate the company, the role, and the manager — using the candidate-question slot for real diligence instead of polite curiosity.

The reframe: interviewing is a two-way conversation. **Refuse the "exam" framing.** Your best signal often comes at the end when the company has dropped its guard and gives more authentic answers — and from *how* they answer, not just *what* they say (hesitation, vague answers, friction around access).

## Key Takeaways

- Treat the candidate-question slot as **due diligence**, not a courtesy formality. Senior candidates have leverage and should use it
- **Promotion velocity, tenure, and "why is this role open?"** are stronger signals about a team's health than any glossy pitch
- Save the highest-stakes questions for **the end** of the loop — guards drop, answers get more honest
- Pay attention to **how** they answer: hesitation, vague answers, or friction around access to teammates is itself the data
- Asking what failure looks like at six months surfaces unscripted truth about role risk that the success-version always hides

## Actionable Insights — The Nine Questions

All nine, verbatim, with what each is designed to surface:

**1.** *"Can you walk me through the last time someone in this team got promoted? What did the promotion look like, and how long had they been here?"*
→ Is progression legible and achievable, or opaque and political?

**2.** *"What's the longest someone in this role has been at the company, and what are they doing now?"*
→ Tenure and stability patterns. Does this seat lead to advancement, or to the exit?

**3.** *"What's something the team is genuinely bad at?"*
→ Self-awareness and honesty vs. humblebrag. A team that can't answer this is either dishonest or hasn't reflected.

**4.** *"How does the team handle disagreement with a decision from leadership?"*
→ Real culture of critical thinking vs. performed alignment.

**5.** *"What's the headcount plan for this team over the next twelve months?"*
→ Trajectory of the team and leadership's clarity about the role's future.

**6.** *"Who would I be working with most closely, and can I speak with them as part of the process?"*
→ Organizational openness. Gatekeeping signals a closed culture; willingness signals confidence.

**7.** *"What does success in this role look like at the six-month mark? And what does failure look like?"*
→ The *failure* half is the unscripted one. Forces them to articulate role risk that the success story always hides.

**8.** *"When was the last reorg, and what triggered it?"*
→ Institutional stability and what leadership actually optimizes for under pressure.

**9.** *"Why is this role open?"*
→ Growth vs. backfill vs. someone-quit-unhappy. The **hesitation** before they answer is itself the signal.

## How to Read the Answers

- **Hesitation, throat-clearing, "let me think about that"** — usually means the honest answer would hurt them. The pause is the signal
- **Vague answers to specific questions** — "we have a strong growth culture" in response to a question about promotion velocity is a soft no
- **Friction around access** — if Q6 (talking to the team) gets pushback, that's almost always a closed-information culture. The role itself will be worse for it
- **Forced positive framing on Q3** ("the team is genuinely bad at" → "I wouldn't say *bad*, more *learning opportunity*") — they can't be honest with you yet, so they won't be honest with you later
